Why Sourcing and Filtering Account for ~65% of Job Search Time
Jun 2026 · Supporting analysis

The 65% figure isn't a gut estimate – it's the result of two compounding forces: a structural floor that exists even under ideal conditions, and a friction multiplier that reflects how job search actually works in practice.

Layer 1: The Structural Floor (~40–45%)

Even in a frictionless world – perfect search results, every posting accurate, zero irrelevant companies – you still cannot apply without first finding and evaluating opportunities. This is unavoidable overhead. Before a single CV is tailored, you must:

  • Identify companies worth applying to
  • Read and parse the job description
  • Cross-reference against your hard stop criteria
  • Verify the role is genuinely open and active

At minimum, this pre-application work accounts for 40–45% of total job search time. This is the structural floor – it doesn't shrink regardless of how efficient you are, because it's not waste; it's the necessary cost of targeting.

Layer 2: The Friction Multiplier

Real-world conditions inflate that floor significantly through three compounding forces.

Ghost Jobs

A substantial share of active LinkedIn postings are not actually active. A 2023 Resume Builder survey found that approximately 40% of companies had posted ghost jobs in the prior year – roles that were filled, frozen, or never intended to be filled, but remained live to build a candidate pipeline. Every ghost job consumed in your sourcing session is dead qualification time.

False Positive Rate

LinkedIn's algorithm optimises for engagement, not precision. A search for "Performance Marketing Manager" in a specific city returns results calibrated to keep you scrolling, not to match your actual criteria. The result is a high false positive rate – you open, read, and disqualify a disproportionate number of JDs before finding a genuine match. For senior or specialised roles where the context (company stage, stack, culture) matters as much as the title, this ratio worsens significantly.

Context Switching Cost

The job search funnel forces constant cognitive mode shifts: broad exploratory search → analytical company evaluation → creative CV tailoring → administrative submission. Gloria Mark's research at UC Irvine found that the average cost of an interrupted task is approximately 23 minutes of refocus time. Each sourcing session generates multiple such switches before a single application is submitted, compounding the time cost invisibly.

The Combined Effect

Starting from a structural floor of ~40–45%, ghost job filtering, false positive culling, and context switching overhead push the realistic total toward 65% – and potentially beyond for senior roles where precision targeting is non-negotiable and the signal-to-noise ratio on LinkedIn is at its lowest.

Personal data. Running weekly job search cycles of 25 hours (5 hours/day, 5 days/week), I tracked where the time actually went. Of those 25 hours, approximately 16.75 hours – 67% – were consumed by sourcing and filtering alone, before a single CV was touched. The remaining 8.25 hours covered tailoring and submission combined. This held consistently across multiple weeks, which is what pushed the estimate toward 65% in the first place.

Conclusion

65% is not a number arrived at by breaking down time and summing to a target. It is the minimum you should expect once you account for the structural cost of finding and qualifying roles, compounded by the friction inherent in how job boards actually work. If anything, for a senior specialist role with precise fit requirements, it is slightly conservative.

Sources

  • 3 in 10 Companies Currently Have Fake Job Postings Listed – ResumeBuilder.com. Survey of 1,641 hiring managers (2023). 40% reported their company had posted a ghost job in the prior year.
  • The Cost of Interrupted Work: More Speed and Stress – Gloria Mark, UC Irvine (CHI 2008). Key finding: after an interruption, it takes an average of 23 minutes and 15 seconds to fully return to the original task.
